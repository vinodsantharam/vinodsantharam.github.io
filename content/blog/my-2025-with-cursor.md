## A year in numbers

Cursor sent me my 2025 wrapped, and it told a story I hadn't quite noticed while living it. I started the year still _typing_ — leaning on autocomplete, finishing my own thoughts, very much in the driver's seat. I ended it letting the Agent do most of the work.

You can read the whole arc in two numbers: **8,532** Tab presses and **616** agent messages. One of those is the engineer I was in spring. The other is the one I'd become by autumn.

So here's my year with Cursor, told the way it actually happened — from Tab to Agent.

## The Tab year

Let me start with my favorite stat, because it's the one I'm a little nostalgic about.

![Tab key presses in Cursor across 2025: 8,532 total, peaking at 3,274 in August](/cursor-tab-2025.svg)

**8,532 presses.** Tab was, by a wide margin, the key I leaned on most. And I loved it — not for any deep reason, but for pure speed and flow. Cursor's autocomplete kept me _in the zone_: it finished the line before I did, I'd glance, hit `Tab`, and keep moving. No context switch, no waiting on a chat, no reading a diff. Just me, typing, with the friction sanded off.

Yes — at that point I was still coding a fair bit myself. Tab wasn't the machine doing the thinking; it was the machine getting out of my way while _I_ did. That distinction mattered to me then, and honestly it still does.

The shape of the chart is the tell. A trickle in May, building through summer, and then **August** — 3,274 presses in a single month, my most intense stretch of the whole year. That was peak Cursor: the most Tab presses, and my longest streak too. After that, the line comes down and never climbs back. Something was shifting.

## Enter the Agent

What was shifting, of course, was that I'd stopped typing as much and started _delegating_.

Across the year I sent **616 messages** to the Agent over **151 chats**, and the curve peaks right alongside the Tab one — July and August were the months I put the Agent to work hardest. I used Cursor on **145 days**, with a longest streak of **13 days**, and — no surprise — that streak landed in August.

That's the quiet plot twist of the whole recap. The two peaks sit back to back: I was tab-completing furiously _and_ leaning on the Agent in the same stretch. August was the month the old way and the new way overlapped — the last big hurrah of me-as-typist, and the first real surge of me-as-orchestrator. Everything after it tapers, because by autumn the Agent was simply doing more of the work than my keyboard was.

## A year of models

The other thing that drifted over the year was _which_ model I was talking to.

[![My Cursor model usage across 2025: GPT 4.1 early in the year, Gemini 2.5 Pro dominating mid-year, then Claude 4 and 4.5 Sonnet later on, with cameos from o3, Composer 1, Auto, and GPT 5.1](/cursor-2025-models.png)](/cursor-2025-models.png)

I opened the year on **GPT 4.1**, but the bulk of the chart belongs to **Gemini 2.5 Pro** — it carried me through the heavy-building summer. I won't pretend it was a rigorous decision: a friend of mine, a director of design who'd caught the vibe-coding bug himself, told me to just try Gemini. I did, it was fast and a great default for the kind of work I was doing, and it stuck for months.

By the back half of the year the needle had swung to **Claude 4 Sonnet** and then **4.5 Sonnet** — better feel for the tougher work, and my top three for the year ended up Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 4 Sonnet, Claude 4.5 Sonnet, in that order. (There were cameos too — o3, Composer 1, Auto, a bit of GPT 5.1.) It mirrors something I keep coming back to: there's no one best model, just the right one for the task in front of you.

Which brings me to the part the chart only hints at.

## Giving Claude Code a chance

If you look at that recap and then at my profile today, you'll notice the line went quiet over the last few months. That's not Cursor's fault — it's me. I gave **Claude Code** a chance, and I haven't really gone back.

It wasn't a dramatic breakup. Cursor was my gateway into AI coding, and I still respect what it does. But the ground kept moving: the skills ecosystem kept growing, my X feed wouldn't stop talking about it, friends kept bringing it up, and a steady diet of Matt Pocock's content nudged me the rest of the way. At some point it just felt like something I _had_ to try — agent-first, terminal-native, skills everywhere.

So I tried it. And the way that experiment reshaped how I think about coding with AI — the fatigue, the discipline, the foundations you can't skip — turned into a whole post of its own: [State of Coding, June 2026](/blog/state-of-coding-june-2026).

But that's the next chapter. This one ends in August 2025, with me hammering `Tab` 3,274 times and not yet knowing it was the high-water mark of typing my own code.
